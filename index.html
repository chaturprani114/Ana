Trade classifications & the rate hierarchy

In construction, pay is an attribute of the work being performed, not the person. Every hour must resolve to the correct rate by cascading through four levels — from the highest-priority Prevailing Wage down to the company default.

Classification levels

Apprentice
In a registered training program. Paid a percentage of the Journeyman rate. 1st year ≈ 50%, 2nd ≈ 65%, 3rd ≈ 80%, 4th ≈ 90%.
Journeyman
Fully qualified worker. This is the base for all wage determinations and apprentice percentage calculations.
Foreman
Supervisory role. Paid a premium above Journeyman rate — typically +$5.00/hr to +$3/hr depending on the CBA.
Gen. Foreman
Senior supervisory. Commands the highest field premium, often +$7–$10/hr above Journeyman rate.

Earnings types & the gross pay calculation

Every paycheck is built from multiple earnings components — straight time, overtime, double time, shift differentials, and per diems. Each has different tax treatment and calculation rules.

Straight time

1× base rate

The first 40 hours worked in a single workweek. All federal and state taxes apply. This is the foundation for all multiplier calculations.

ST Pay = Base Rate × Regular Hours

Overtime

1.5× base rate

Required by federal FLSA for hours over 40 in a workweek. Some CBAs require daily OT after 8 hours. On PW jobs, OT is on the BASE rate — fringe stays flat.

OT Pay = Base Rate × 1.5 × OT Hours

Double time

2× base rate

Required in California after 12 hours in a single shift, or for the 7th consecutive day. Also required on most Sundays and holidays under many CBAs.

DT Pay = Base Rate × 2.0 × DT Hours

Per diems & travel (non-taxable)

Per diems are non-taxable if they follow IRS "Accountable Plan" rules — workers must be traveling away from their tax home for business.

GSA Lodging (2026)
$110/day standard CONUS rate. Actual hotel receipt required, or use the flat rate.
GSA M&IE (2026)
$68/day for meals & incidentals. Reduced to 75% ($51) on first and last day of travel.
IRS Mileage (2026)
$0.725/mile for personal vehicle use. Tax-free to the employee. No receipt required.
Company Vehicle
No reimbursement owed, but personal use value may be taxable income to the employee.
If a worker eats lunch while "on call" or at their desk, that meal is taxable — it doesn't qualify as a per diem allowance.

Pay period & pay day rules

Weekly (field standard)

Most states require weekly pay for field construction laborers. A 4–5 day lag from pay period close to pay day is normal and legal — allows time for auditing job codes.

W-2 Employee

The company controls the work, provides tools/direction, and withholds taxes. All field laborers who work under supervision should be W-2.

1099 Warning

Misclassifying field laborers as 1099 contractors to avoid taxes is a major legal risk — IRS, DOL, and state agencies all audit this aggressively.

Benefits, fringes & the TPA process

In construction, benefits are not simple payroll deductions — they are contractual obligations managed by Third-Party Administrators (TPAs) and Union Trusts. Understanding the total package is critical for job costing and compliance.

Benefit types

Health & Welfare
Medical, dental, and vision premiums. Both employer and employee contribute. Often the largest fringe line item.
Pension / Annuity
Long-term retirement fund. Usually employer-paid per hour worked — e.g., $6.50/hr regardless of classification.
401(k)
Employee saves pre-tax (Traditional) or post-tax (Roth). Employer often matches a percentage of contribution.
Training Fund
Employer-paid contributions to apprenticeship and trade school programs. Usually $0.25–$1.00/hr worked.
Garnishments
Involuntary legal deductions — child support, tax levies, creditor judgments. Employee-paid, must be processed in specific priority order.

401(k) deep dive

Traditional (Pre-Tax)
Contributions reduce taxable income today. Taxes paid at withdrawal during retirement. Best when current tax rate > expected retirement rate.
Roth (Post-Tax)
Contributions are taxed now, but all withdrawals at retirement are tax-free. Best when current rate < expected retirement rate.
Vesting Schedule
Employee's own contributions vest immediately. Employer match vests over time — e.g., 20% per year for 5 years. Worker who leaves early forfeits unvested portion.

Prevailing Wage fringe offset ("Cash in Lieu")

Davis-Bacon requires a total package (base + fringe). If the employer's existing benefits don't cover the full required fringe, the remainder must be paid in cash — which is taxable income.

The tax web: federal, state & local

Construction workers cross jurisdictional lines constantly. A single worker can owe taxes to the federal government, their work state, their home state, and multiple cities or school districts — all in a single week.

Federal taxes

FIT
Federal Income Tax. Progressive rate, employee-paid, withheld by employer based on W-4 elections.
Social Security
6.2% employee + 6.2% employer on first $168,600 of wages (2024 wage base). Both sides flow to SSA.
Medicare
1.45% employee + 1.45% employer on all wages. Additional 0.9% on wages over $200,000 (employee only).
FUTA
6.0% employer-only on first $7,000 of wages. Reduced to 0.6% effective rate if SUTA is paid timely.
SUTA
State unemployment — employer-only. Rate driven by the company's "experience rating" (layoff history). Varies 0.1%–10%+.

State & local tax layers

State income tax (work state)
Primary obligation — where the work is physically performed. Rates vary widely: 0% (TX, FL) to 13.3% (CA top bracket).
City / municipal tax (work city)
You almost always owe tax to the city where you physically perform work — e.g., Philadelphia 3.75%, NYC 3.876%. Withheld at source.
Home city / county tax
Your residence city may also want a cut. A credit system prevents full double-taxation — if you pay 2% to work city, home city credits that amount.
School district tax (residence)
Ohio, PA, and others levy LSD/CSD tax based purely on where the worker sleeps — not where they work. Must update when worker moves.
Reciprocity agreements
Neighboring states (e.g., NJ ↔ PA, MD ↔ DC, KY ↔ OH) agree to collect only one state's tax, preventing double-taxation for commuters.

Unions & Collective Bargaining Agreements (CBAs)

Unions represent craft workers and negotiate legally binding contracts — CBAs — that dictate exactly what a worker must be paid, what benefits must be provided, and what working conditions must be maintained.

How unions work

1
Workers join a Local
A "Local" is a regional chapter — e.g., IBEW Local 98 (Philadelphia). Each local negotiates its own wage scales.
2
Local negotiates a CBA
The union negotiates with an Employer Association (a group of contractors). The resulting CBA is a legally binding contract for all signatory employers.
3
CBA sets all payroll terms
Wages by classification, fringe rates (H&W, pension, training), OT/DT triggers, shift differentials, and work rules are all specified.
4
Employer remits to Trust Funds
Employer fringe contributions go to union trust funds (H&W trust, pension trust, training trust), managed by trustees — not the union itself.

Union fees & deductions

Working Dues
A percentage of gross pay (typically 1.5–3%) deducted from the worker's check and remitted monthly to the union. This is employee-paid.
Initiation Fee
One-time fee when a worker joins the local. Often paid in installments through payroll deductions.
Employer Fringes
Amounts paid by the employer (not deducted from worker) into H&W, pension, annuity, and training trust funds per hour worked.
Union Traveler
A member of Local 1 working in Local 2's jurisdiction. Benefits must be reciprocated — hours and contributions sent back to the worker's home local.
A "Union Traveler" scenario requires the payroll system to know which local the worker belongs to and route fringe remittances to the correct trust fund — often in a different city or state.

Major construction unions at a glance

IBEW

Job costing & accounting integration

Every dollar of labor cost must be assigned to a Project and Cost Code. This is how contractors know which jobs are profitable, and it's what drives the General Ledger entries for financial reporting.

Labor burden — the real cost of an hour

The invoice rate charged to a job is not the worker's gross wage — it's the fully burdened cost including all taxes and benefits the employer pays.

Breaks, rounding & wage & hour compliance

Time calculation is not just a productivity tool — it's a legal minefield. Improper break tracking and rounding policies are among the most common sources of wage & hour lawsuits in construction.

Meal breaks (unpaid)

Standard window
30-minute unpaid break where the worker is completely relieved of duty. Can be deducted from total hours.
"On-call" exception
If the worker eats while monitoring equipment or must be ready to respond, the break must be paid as hours worked.
CA penalty rule
If a meal break is not provided before the 5th hour of work, the employer owes 1 hour of "premium pay" at the regular rate. Per shift, not per week.
2nd meal break (CA)
Must be provided before the 10th hour of work. If waived by employee (and shift is under 12 hrs), must be in writing.
California meal break penalties can stack quickly — 3 workers × 5 days × $45/hr penalty = $675 in premium pay owed before attorney fees.

Rest breaks (paid)

Duration
10–15 minutes per 4 hours worked. These count as "hours worked" and are never deducted from timecards.
Frequency
1 rest break per 4-hour period. An 8-hour shift requires 2 rest breaks. A 10-hour shift requires 2–3 depending on state.
Penalty (CA)
If a rest break is not provided, the employer owes 1 hour of premium pay — same as the meal break penalty.

Time rounding rules

15-min rounding
Most common policy. 8:07 AM rounds to 8:00; 8:08 AM rounds to 8:15. Must be applied consistently — cannot always round in the employer's favor.
DOL audit rule
Over time, rounding must be neutral (benefit employee and employer roughly equally). Consistent employer-favoring rounding is a FLSA violation.
Audit your rounding quarterly. If the average rounding always reduces hours slightly, your policy is non-compliant.

Compliance reporting formats

Construction payroll generates more compliance reports than almost any other industry. Each report serves a specific regulatory audience — federal, state, union, or tax authority.

WH-347 — Certified Payroll

Weekly · Federal projects

Required on all federal and federally-assisted construction projects. Must be filed weekly, typically by Wednesday for the previous week's work.

Who files
Prime contractors and all subcontractors on covered projects. Must include all laborers and mechanics.
What's included
Worker name, address, SSN (partial), work classification, daily hours, total hours, rate of pay, gross wages, deductions, net pay, and fringe benefits paid.
Statement of Compliance
A signed declaration that the payroll is correct and complete. Falsifying a WH-347 is a federal crime — punishable by fines and debarment.
Debarment = being prohibited from bidding on future federal projects. Even a single violation can result in debarment for up to 3 years.

Other key reports

Form 941
Quarterly employer federal tax return. Reports total wages paid, FIT withheld, Social Security and Medicare for the quarter. Due April 30, July 31, Oct 31, Jan 31.
W-2 (Annual)
Annual summary of all wages and withholdings per employee. Must be mailed by January 31. Includes Box 12 codes for benefits (401k, H&W, etc.).
Union Remittance
Monthly CSV to the union hall listing each member, hours worked, and fringe dollars owed. Late remittance triggers interest and audit rights.
SUTA Filing
Quarterly state unemployment wage report. Each state has its own form and portal. Timely filing preserves the FUTA credit (reduces rate from 6% to 0.6%).
1099-NEC
Annual report of payments to contractors. Required for any non-employee paid $600+ in the year. File by January 31 with IRS and recipient.

Annual compliance calendar

January 31
W-2s to employees · 1099-NECs to contractors · Q4 Form 941 due · Q4 SUTA due
February 28 / March 31
W-2 Copy A to SSA (paper Feb 28 · electronic Mar 31) · ACA reporting (1095-C) due
April 30
Q1 Form 941 due · Q1 SUTA due · First FUTA deposit if liability > $500
Monthly (ongoing)
Union remittance reports due — typically by the 15th of the following month. Late = interest.
Weekly (ongoing)
WH-347 certified payroll for each active federal/state prevailing wage project. Cannot be batched.
Semi-weekly or monthly
Federal tax deposits (941 taxes). Frequency determined by lookback period liability. Most construction companies deposit semi-weekly.
